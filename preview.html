MHPAEA: New Parity Rules on Pause

• Background: On May 15, 2025, federal agencies announced they will not enforce the new MHPAEA rules issued Sept. 2024 while litigation (ERIC) is reviewed and potential updates are evaluated.

• Existing obligations remain: 2013 final MHPAEA rules, CAA 2021 parity provisions, and existing guidance (e.g., FAQs Part 45).

• Core parity principle: MH/SUD cost-sharing, QTLs, and NQTLs must be no more restrictive than medical/surgical in the same classification.

• Applies to: Employers with >50 employees sponsoring group health plans covering MH/SUD benefits.

• Timing: New 2024 rule enforcement paused at least through 2025–2026 (plus 18 months after final court decision per agencies).

4

MHPAEA: Go Deeper (What’s Paused vs. Still In Effect)

• Paused (new 2024 Final Rule elements):

– Fiduciary certification confirming plan’s comparative analysis review

– Expanded NQTL comparative analysis content/requirements

– “Meaningful benefits” standard; discriminatory factors/evidentiary standards; new data evaluation requirements

• Agencies’ statement: No enforcement of new 2024 elements prior to final litigation decision + 18 months; statutory MHPAEA (as amended by CAA 2021) still applies.

• Still required now: 2013 MHPAEA final rules; CAA 2021 parity obligations; adherence to existing guidance and ability to produce a compliant comparative analysis upon request.

• High‑risk areas for parity scrutiny: Autism spectrum disorder, eating disorders, medication‑assisted treatment for opioid use disorder.

5

MHPAEA: Employer Implications & Actions

Action Items

• Confirm plan compliance with 2013 MHPAEA rules and CAA 2021 requirements.

• Review current comparative analyses for QTLs, FRs, and NQTLs; remediate gaps.

• Engage TPAs/PBMs and legal counsel on high‑risk benefit areas (ASD, eating disorders, MAT for OUD).

• Document parity rationales for prior authorization, step therapy, and network design (apply evidentiary standards consistently).

• Monitor litigation outcome and agency guidance for timing of any new requirements.

Focus on operational readiness and defendable comparative analyses even while new elements are paused.
  • Update internal parity inventory
  • Coordinate with vendors on NQTL justifications
  • Train benefits staff on current MHPAEA standards
  • Set monitoring cadence for agency updates

“One, Big, Beautiful Bill” (House‑passed; in Senate)

• Background: Introduced May 13, 2025 by House Ways & Means; advances extensions of 2017 tax cuts and multiple benefits provisions; passed House; now in Senate.

• Status: Not law; subject to changes in Senate and conference.

• Applies to: Employers offering group health plans, HSAs/FSAs/HRAs, PFML/child‑care credits, education assistance, ICHRA programs.

• Timing: Provisions signal congressional interests for 2026 and beyond.

7

Bill: Go Deeper (Key Benefit Provisions)

• ICHRAs → “CHOICE Arrangements”: 60‑day notice; W‑2 reporting; allow pre‑tax Exchange premiums; 2‑year small employer credit for new arrangements.

• Tax credits: Updates to PFML credit and employer child‑care credit.

• Education assistance: Index $5,250 limit; permanently allow student loan repayment reimbursement.

• HSAs/FSAs highlights:

– 60 days to establish HSA; retroactive eligibility to QHDHP start date

– HSA‑compatibility expansions (e.g., Part A, certain Exchange plans, limited clinics)

– DPC HSA‑compatibility if under capped fee and limited services; DPC fees as §213(d)

– Fitness/sports expenses HSA‑reimbursable (annual caps, indexed)

– Both spouses can make catch‑ups to same HSA; added HSA contributions with income phase‑outs

– FSA/HRA to HSA conversions with lookback conditions; spouse FSA no longer HSA‑disqualifying

8

Bill: Employer Implications & Planning Signals

Action Items

• Track Senate activity; prepare for potential 2026 plan design updates.

• Model CHOICE/ICHRA options, tax credit eligibility (non‑ALE), and W‑2 reporting needs.

• Coordinate with payroll/HRIS for possible HSA/FSA changes (eligibility, contributions, DPC, fitness).

• Review education assistance programs for indexing and student loan repayment design.

• Consult legal/tax advisors; confirm cafeteria plan and HSA policy updates if enacted.

While not yet law, provisions indicate likely areas of change. Early modeling helps ensure smooth adoption if enacted.
  • Engage brokers/TPAs on scenario planning
  • Draft conditional plan language
  • Budget for system/config changes
  • Employee comms plan for 2026

Executive Order: “Most‑Favored‑Nation” Drug Pricing (May 12, 2025)

• Background: Strategy aims for U.S. drug prices comparable to other developed countries; could reshape pricing and delivery channels.

• Applies to: All employers sponsoring plans with Rx benefits due to potential downstream pricing and access changes.

• Status: Initial focus (May 20) on brand drugs without generic/biosimilar competition; legal challenges expected; durable changes likely require Congress.

10

Executive Order: Go Deeper (Agency Actions)

• Establish most‑favored‑nation pricing: Announce targeted prices; propose rulemaking; potential FDA pressure (modify/revoke certain approvals).

• Address anti‑competitive behaviors: Investigate foreign exporters and drug supply chain practices; enforce against anti‑competitive conduct.

• Direct‑to‑consumer sales: Allow manufacturers agreeing to MFN pricing to sell/ship directly to consumers.

• Drug importation: Increase flexibility when manufacturers do not align with MFN pricing.

11

Executive Order: Employer Implications

Action Items

• Monitor litigation and agency rulemaking timelines impacting Rx unit costs and distribution.

• Ask PBMs about exposure to brand drugs without generic/biosimilar competition.

• Review plan and stop‑loss impacts of potential price shifts or direct‑to‑consumer channels.

• Consider international reference pricing clauses or outcomes‑based strategies where feasible.

Changes could be significant but uncertain. Prepare for scenario impacts on formularies, member access, and plan costs.
  • Quarterly PBM check‑ins
  • Model high‑cost drug exposures
  • Member communication templates
  • Legal review for DTC/importation

2026 HSA, HDHP & EBHRA Limits (Rev. Proc. 2025‑19)

• Background: IRS announced 2026 COLAs for HSA, HDHP, EBHRA; all 2025 limits increase except HSA catch‑up ($1,000 unchanged).

• Applies to: Employers offering HDHP/HSA or EBHRA benefits; payroll/HRIS for 2026 setup.

• Timing: HSA contribution limits = calendar year; HDHP/EBHRA limits apply to plan years beginning in 2026.

• Notes: Embedded family deductible must meet minimum family deductible; non‑grandfathered plans have per‑person OOP max requirements.

CMS: 2026 Medicare Part D Parameters & Methodology Update

• Background: CMS released 2026 Part D benefit parameters and revised simplified creditable coverage determination methodology.

• Applies to: All employers sponsoring Rx coverage (including COBRA/retiree participants) that must determine and notify creditability.

• Timing: Revised simplified method available in 2026; current simplified method expected to sunset in 2027.

17

Part D: Go Deeper (Key 2026 Changes)

• Deductible increases from $590 → $615; OOP max increases from $2,000 → $2,100.

• Revised simplified determination adds: reasonable coverage of biologics; removes a deductible test; raises expected plan payment threshold from 60% → 72%.

• Practical: Aligns with Inflation Reduction Act enhancements; some plans may need actuarial review to confirm creditability.

18

Part D: Employer Implications & Next Steps

Action Items

• Work with carriers/PBMs to determine 2026 creditability under revised simplified method.

• Engage actuary if carrier/PBM cannot certify; consider design changes to meet 72% expectation and biologics coverage.

• Prepare and distribute 2026 creditable/non‑creditable notices on time (including COBRA/retirees).

• Plan for 2027: current simplified method expected to be unavailable—ensure future compliance.

Early assessment avoids last‑minute notices and helps align plan value with updated CMS expectations.
  • Request 2026 creditability from PBM
  • Obtain actuarial memo if needed
  • Update Rx plan designs as needed
  • Finalize Part D notices workflow

RxDC Reporting: Due Sunday, June 1, 2025 (CY 2024 Data)

• Background: CAA 2021 §204 requires annual medical/Rx data reporting (RxDC) to federal government.

• Applies to: All employers sponsoring a group medical plan and Rx benefit (all sizes).

• Exempt: Excepted benefits (stand‑alone dental/vision), FSAs, HSAs, HRAs, ICHRAs.

• Process: Carriers/TPAs typically submit most files; employers provide employer/employee premium shares.

21

RxDC: Go Deeper (Data & Self‑Funded Calculations)

• Employer inputs: Prior CY employer vs. employee contributions (incl. COBRA) to medical/Rx.

• Self‑funded “premium equivalent” = actual fixed costs + actual claims (choose incurred or paid for CY; be consistent) − stop‑loss rebates − pharmacy rebates retained by plan; employer paid = premium equivalent − employee contributions.

• Missed outreach: If employer doesn’t respond, carrier/TPA submits most data; employer must fill gaps via HIOS or engage a vendor.

• Tip: CMS RxDC webpage provides current instructions/templates.

22

RxDC: Employer Actions Now

Action Items

• Confirm who is filing which files (carrier/TPA vs. employer) and internal deadlines.

• Provide contribution splits (employer vs. employee) by carrier deadlines.

• For self‑funded: Calculate premium equivalent per methodology and document approach (incurred vs. paid).

• If gaps remain, submit employer data in HIOS or engage vendor support.

This is the 4th cycle—leverage improved carrier/TPA processes, but verify accountability and deadlines.
  • Identify responsible contacts
  • Archive methodology and proofs
  • Set reminder for next cycle prep
  • Use CMS resources/checklists

PCORI Fees: Due July 31, 2025 (Report on Q2 Form 720)

• Background: Annual fee funds Patient‑Centered Outcomes Research Institute; due by July 31 each year on Q2 Form 720.

• Applies to: Self‑funded medical (incl. level‑funded), HRA paired with fully insured, ICHRA. Carriers pay for fully insured plans.

• Exempt: Excepted benefits (stand‑alone dental/vision), HSAs, health FSAs.

• 2025 Form 720 released early; employers can file/pay anytime before July 31.

24

PCORI: Go Deeper (Rates & Counting Methods)

• Rates for plan years ending in 2024: $3.22 per covered life (plan years ending before Oct 1, 2024); $3.47 per covered life (ending Oct 1–Dec 31, 2024).

• Covered lives: Employees + spouses + dependents (HRAs use enrolled employees only).

• Counting methods: Actual Count; Snapshot; Snapshot Factor (2.35 factor for other‑than‑self‑only); Form 5500 method (if filed by July 31, 2025).

• Late payments: Use prior‑year forms; IRS may allow reasonable cause appeals if penalized.

25

PCORI: Employer Action Checklist

Action Items

• Obtain average covered lives from TPA (or calculate via approved method).

• Complete Q2 Form 720; pay electronically or by voucher (Form 720‑V) by July 31.

• If using Form 5500 method, ensure filing (or extension) timing supports July 31 deadline.

• Retain documentation: method used, counts, calculation, and payment confirmation.

Only file Form 720 for Q2 if PCORI is your sole excise tax; no filings are needed for other quarters.
  • Confirm plan year end date
  • Validate rate ($3.22 vs. $3.47)
  • Set internal approval workflow
  • Archive proof of payment

QOTM: Correcting FSA Administrative Errors

• Background: No formal IRS correction rules; unofficial guidance allows correction with “clear and convincing” evidence of employer administrative error.

• Scenarios: Eligible employee excluded; ineligible employee enrolled; reimbursement of ineligible expenses (error discovered before vs. after plan year end).

• Applies to: Employers administering health/dependent care FSAs (and vendors/TPAs supporting them).

28

FSA Corrections: Go Deeper (Common Fixes)

• Eligible but excluded: Consider retro enrollment with prospective salary reductions—or provide taxable compensation to offset after‑tax costs.

• Ineligible but enrolled: Reverse participation as if never enrolled; reconcile contributions vs. reimbursements; settle amounts owed/owed back (refund taxable).

• Ineligible reimbursements (error found before year end): Deactivate card; demand repayment; withhold from pay if permitted; offset against future valid claims; if unrecovered, treat as other business indebtedness (W‑2 if forgiven).

• Ineligible reimbursements (after year end): Treat as business indebtedness; if forgiven, include as taxable wages in year of forgiveness.

29

FSA Errors: Employer Action Checklist

Action Items

• Establish written correction procedures referencing IRS informal guidance and payroll rules.

• Coordinate with TPAs on card deactivation, repayments, offsets, and documentation.

• Confirm state wage withholding permissibility; align SPD/plan language and card terms.

• Determine tax reporting for forgiven indebtedness (Form W‑2) when applicable.

• Maintain evidence of the error and corrective action (“clear and convincing”).

Choose the correction path that is administratively feasible and fairest to the employee while maintaining plan compliance.
  • Document error and root cause
  • Update SOPs to prevent recurrence
  • Consult counsel on complex cases
  • Communicate resolution to employee
